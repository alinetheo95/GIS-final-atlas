NYC's Urban Mycelium

What if New York City's street grid grew organically, like mycelium networks beneath the forest floor? Let's explore the layers of natural and built systems that shape the city.

The Commissioner's Plan

In 1811, New York adopted its famous grid plan. This rigid geometric pattern would shape Manhattan's development for centuries, prioritizing efficiency over the island's natural topography.

Population Density

Today, this grid holds one of the world's densest populations. The darker areas show neighborhoods with over 100,000 people per square mile.

The Built Environment

Layered on top of the grid are impervious surfaces - buildings, roads, and infrastructure that prevent water from reaching the ground below.

Urban Heat Islands

What Came Before

Before the grid, Manhattan was a landscape of forests, meadows, and wetlands. This layer shows the historical ecology that once flourished here.

Then and Now

Overlaying the historical ecology with today's buildings reveals just how much has been transformed in 200 years.

Buried Waterways

Manhattan once had dozens of streams and creeks flowing to both rivers. Most are now buried beneath streets and buildings.

Hidden Infrastructure

These buried streams still exist as underground pipes and sewers, occasionally flooding basements and subway tunnels during heavy rains.

Lost Wetlands

Wetlands once covered much of Manhattan's edges and low-lying areas, providing natural flood protection and wildlife habitat.

Natural Corridors

Together, the streams and wetlands formed a natural network - much like the mycelium networks that connect trees in a forest.

Today's Urban Forest

Despite the density, NYC maintains over 600,000 street trees. Each green point represents a tree in the city's inventory.

Green Infrastructure

These trees provide crucial ecosystem services: cooling the city, filtering air pollution, managing stormwater, and connecting wildlife corridors.

Imagining Alternatives

What if the city's streets had followed these natural systems? What if, instead of a rigid grid, our infrastructure grew organically like mycelium - adapting to topography, following water, connecting green spaces?
